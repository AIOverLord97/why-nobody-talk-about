Why nobody talks about the person who coined "virtual reality"

Published on: October 12, 2025 | Category: People & Geniuses

Before Mark Zuckerberg rebranded Facebook as Meta and promised to build the metaverse, before Palmer Luckey launched Oculus and sold it for billions, there was Jaron Lanier—a brilliant, eccentric visionary who not only coined the term "virtual reality" but built the first commercial VR systems in the 1980s. His story is one of extraordinary prescience, missed opportunities, and the strange fate of being the prophet who saw the future but couldn't control it.

The Rise

Jaron Lanier emerged from the counterculture of 1980s California with a vision that seemed like science fiction. A classically trained musician and computer programmer, he founded VPL Research in 1984 to commercialize virtual reality technology. The company's name stood for "Visual Programming Language," reflecting Lanier's belief that computing should be visual and intuitive.

Lanier didn't just build VR hardware; he created the entire concept and vocabulary. He coined the term "virtual reality" to describe immersive, computer-generated environments. His company developed the DataGlove (which allowed users to manipulate virtual objects), the EyePhone (a head-mounted display), and the first full-body motion capture suit. These weren't just prototypes—VPL sold them to NASA, Boeing, and other major organizations.

At its peak in the late 1980s, VPL was the center of the VR universe. Lanier appeared on the cover of magazines, consulted for Hollywood films, and demonstrated VR technology that wouldn't become mainstream for another 30 years. He wasn't just building technology; he was creating a new medium for human experience.

The Peak

VPL's technology was revolutionary for its time. The company held fundamental patents for VR interfaces, motion capture, and haptic feedback. Lanier and his team were solving problems that would take the industry decades to rediscover: latency reduction, 3D interface design, and creating compelling virtual experiences.

Lanier's vision extended far beyond technology. He saw VR as a tool for communication, education, and artistic expression. He talked about "post-symbolic communication" where people could share experiences directly rather than through language. His ideas anticipated many concepts that would later become central to discussions about the metaverse and digital identity.

By 1990, VPL had raised millions in venture capital and was collaborating with major corporations. The future seemed limitless—VR was going to transform everything from entertainment to medicine to education. Lanier was the charismatic leader of what appeared to be the next computing revolution.

The Fall

The collapse came swiftly. VPL was spending money faster than it could generate revenue, and the technology wasn't yet ready for mass adoption. The hardware was expensive, the graphics were primitive by today's standards, and the "VR boom" of the early 1990s turned out to be premature.

In 1992, VPL filed for bankruptcy. The company's assets were sold off, and its patents ended up scattered across the industry. Lanier lost control of the technology he had invented and the vision he had articulated. The VR winter had begun, and it would last for two decades.

As VR faded from public consciousness, Lanier's role as its primary visionary was largely forgotten. When VR reemerged in the 2010s with Oculus and other companies, a new generation of entrepreneurs and investors took credit for "inventing" virtual reality, unaware of Lanier's pioneering work decades earlier.

The Legacy

Despite VPL's failure, Lanier's conceptual contributions endure. The term "virtual reality" is now ubiquitous, and many of his ideas about digital identity, avatars, and immersive computing have become standard concepts in the industry. His early work laid the foundation for everything that followed.

Lanier reinvented himself as a critic and philosopher of technology. His books, including "You Are Not a Gadget" and "Who Owns the Future?," offer sharp critiques of social media, big data, and the attention economy. He became one of technology's most thoughtful critics, warning about the dangers of the very digital world he helped create.

Perhaps most importantly, Lanier demonstrated that inventing a technology doesn't mean you control its future. His story shows how visionaries can create entire fields, only to see them developed and commercialized by others with different values and priorities.

Closing Thoughts

Jaron Lanier represents the archetype of the visionary who is both ahead of his time and ultimately displaced by it. He saw the potential of virtual reality decades before the technology caught up, but he couldn't maintain control of the revolution he started.

What makes Lanier's story particularly fascinating is his transformation from technology evangelist to technology critic. Having helped create the digital world, he became one of its most eloquent critics, warning about the dangers of social media, surveillance capitalism, and the loss of human agency. His journey reflects the broader technology industry's transition from optimism to concern about its own creations.

Today, as billions of dollars pour into the metaverse and VR finally approaches mainstream adoption, it's worth remembering the eccentric visionary who first imagined these digital worlds. Lanier's story reminds us that technological progress is often messy and unpredictable, and that the people who invent new technologies don't always get to shape how they're used. His legacy is both the virtual reality we're building today and the warnings about what it might become.
